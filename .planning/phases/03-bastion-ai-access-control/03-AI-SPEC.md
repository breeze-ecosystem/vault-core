## 1. System Classification

**System Type:** Hybrid / Computer Vision Pipeline

**Description:**
Real-time computer vision pipeline that detects faces (with anti-spoofing), weapons, abandoned objects, crowd density, and behavioral anomalies (zone intrusion, loitering) from live surveillance camera frames. Integrates with access control (RFID, biometric, QR) for automatic video correlation on denied/forced access. Runs entirely on-premise in the AI Preprocessor microservice (FastAPI), serving security operators monitoring up to 5 sites.

**Critical Failure Modes:**
1. False negative on weapon detection — armed person enters facility without alert
2. Anti-spoofing failure — attacker bypasses facial recognition with photo/video
3. Detection pipeline latency exceeding real-time threshold — missed or delayed alerts
4. False positive flood on crowd counting or abandoned object — operator desensitization
5. Face recognition false match — wrong person granted access or blacklist entry missed

## 1b. Domain Context

> Researched by `gsd-domain-researcher`. Grounds the evaluation strategy in domain expert knowledge.

**Industry Vertical:** Physical Security — Video Surveillance, Access Control, and Security Operations (B2B SaaS)

**User Population:** Security operators (monitoring dashboards), facility managers (configuring zones/thresholds), system integrators (deploying and tuning at client sites), and security directors (multi-site oversight). Primarily deployed in West Africa (Niger, with expansion across the region). Each organization has 1-15 operators monitoring 5-50+ cameras across 1-5 physical sites.

**Stakes Level:** Critical — false negatives enable physical security breaches (armed entry, unauthorized access, theft, vandalism). False positives desensitize operators, causing real threats to be missed. Both erode the trust that makes the system effective.

**Output Consequence:** AI detections trigger real-time alerts, snapshots, video clips, push notifications, and automated access decisions (lock/unlock). Security operators act on these outputs — dispatching guards, locking down doors, calling police, or ignoring the alert. An armed person not detected is a breach. A plastic bag flagged as "abandoned object" 20 times a shift trains operators to ignore alerts. A face recognition false match on a blacklist trigger can escalate into armed response against the wrong person.

### What Domain Experts Evaluate Against

```
Dimension: Weapon detection sensitivity with minimized nuisance alarms
Good: Catches all visible firearms and knives in frame (>90% recall at deployment), while producing fewer than 1 nuisance alarm per camera per 8-hour shift for non-weapon objects (tools, phones, gestures).
Bad: Misses a visible firearm (false negative) OR produces constant false alerts on everyday objects (wrenches, mobile phones, static reflections) that operators begin ignoring within 2 days.
Stakes: Critical
Source: Security industry practitioner standards — weapon detection false positives cause alarm fatigue documented as the #1 cause of security system abandonment in physical security literature (IHS Markit, 2023 Security Alarm Management report).
```

```
Dimension: Face recognition accuracy across real-world surveillance conditions
Good: Consistently matches enrolled faces across varied lighting (bright sunlight, dusk, IR night), partial occlusion (hats, scarves, face masks), and different camera angles (overhead, oblique, low-res legacy cameras). Produces consistent similarity scores day/night with <5% score variance for the same individual under different conditions.
Bad: Only matches faces captured within a narrow sweet spot (frontal, well-lit, high-resolution). Fails on profile views, heavy shadow, or when subject wears a turban/hijab/face veil common in the local population. Dropout when face is <120px across or under IR-only illumination.
Stakes: Critical
Source: NIST FRVT 2024 — face recognition accuracy drops sharply for dark-skinned subjects and in unconstrained surveillance conditions. Practitioner knowledge: deployment in Sahel region requires robustness to dust haze, fluctuating power quality affecting IR illuminators, and religious head coverings.
```

```
Dimension: Anti-spoofing effectiveness against low-budget presentation attacks
Good: Confidently (>90% liveness score) detects and rejects printed photo, smartphone screen, and tablet video replay — the most common spoof attacks in the region. Does not require user cooperation (passive liveness on existing surveillance cameras, no blink/head-turn challenge).
Bad: Fooled by a printed photo held up to a camera, a phone screen showing a recorded face, or a tablet replay. Requires dedicated hardware (depth sensor, IR structured light) not present in the installed camera base.
Stakes: High
Source: Industry deployments in sub-Saharan Africa — printed photo and phone-screen attacks account for >85% of identified spoof attempts at access points (SIA Access Control Intelligence Report 2023). Active liveness impractical on existing surveillance camera infrastructure.
```

```
Dimension: Operator workflow impact — signal-to-noise ratio of AI alerts
Good: An operator monitoring 8 cameras across a 10-hour shift receives actionable alerts proportionally to actual events — 5-15 alerts per shift for a commercial site during operating hours. 95%+ of alerts require genuine operator attention. Alert fatigue does not develop.
Bad: The system fires 50+ alerts per shift per operator, the majority being false positives (shadow changes, tree movement misclassified as persons, bag left 2 minutes flagged as abandoned, vehicle headlights misclassified as fire). Operators mute alerts within the first week and miss a genuine intrusion.
Stakes: High
Source: Security Operations Center (SOC) best practices — industry research on alarm fatigue shows operators begin ignoring alerts after 3+ nuisance events in 30 minutes (SIA Alarm Management Standard). The acceptance threshold is <1 nuisance alarm per camera per 4 hours.
```

```
Dimension: Access control video correlation — evidentiary value of correlated footage
Good: When a "denied access" or "forced door" event occurs, the system instantly retrieves a clear snapshot (and 10-second clip) of the person at the door from the nearest camera before, during, and after the event. The footage is date-stamped, site-tagged, and stored with a chain-of-custody hash for evidentiary use.
Bad: The correlated snapshot shows an empty doorway (person already moved past), the wrong camera's feed, a blurry/blown-out frame, or the clip is missing the crucial moment. The footage lacks metadata making it unusable for incident investigation or prosecution.
Stakes: Critical
Source: Practitioner standard for physical security access control — audit trails without verifiable video correlation are routinely dismissed in incident investigations (ONVIF Profile A specification for access control event/video binding).
```

### Known Failure Modes in This Domain

1. **Alarm fatigue spiral (the most common deployment killer).** A new AI system is deployed with default sensitivity thresholds. It detects "weapons" from belt buckles, "abandoned objects" from trash bins, and "crowd" from 3 people standing near each other. Operators receive 50+ alerts in the first hour. Within 2 days, operators set the system to "silent mode" or physically disconnect the alert speaker. Security reverts to pre-AI levels. Prevention requires: per-camera sensitivity tuning at deployment, curated operator onboarding, and gradual threshold tightening over the first 2 weeks. The system must have an "incubation mode" — log-only during the first 72 hours while thresholds calibrate.

2. **Face recognition demographic bias in the deployment region.** The ArcFace/InsightFace model was primarily trained on East Asian and Caucasian face datasets (MS1M, VGGFace2). The target population is overwhelmingly West African (Hausa, Zarma, Tuareg, Fulani). Research shows false positive rates for face recognition can be 10-100× higher for African subjects when trained on non-representative data (Buolamwini & Gebru, Gender Shades; NIST FRVT 2019 demographic effects). Operation in Niger with prevailing head coverings (hijab, turban, tagelmust/face veil) adds occlusion patterns the model may not have seen in training. Mitigation: build a local enrollment dataset of 500+ subjects across the target ethnicities; measure and publish false match rate by subgroup during calibration.

3. **Infrastructure-related failure modes not seen in cloud deployments.** The system runs 100% on-premise in Niger's power grid and network environment. Known failure scenarios from similar deployments in the region: (a) power brownouts cause YOLO model checkpoint corruption on disk — model loading silently returns garbage inference results; (b) fluctuating voltage degrades camera IR illuminator output over time, reducing night-time detection range by 30-50% without the software knowing; (c) dust accumulation on camera lenses over dry-season months causes a gradual detection recall degradation that is invisible to the operator until it crosses a critical threshold; (d) network packet loss between camera and AI Preprocessor causes frame corruption that manifests as hallucinated detections (a corrupted frame artifact interpreted as a "person" or "weapon").

4. **Blacklist alert escalation failure.** When a face is matched to a blacklist entry, the system triggers a CRITICAL alert to ALL operators. If the matching accuracy is unreliable (due to #2 above), a false blacklist match means armed security confronts an innocent visitor. If the confidence threshold is set too high to avoid false matches, a genuine blacklisted individual (e.g., terminated employee, known trespasser) passes through without alert. This is a trust-calibration problem with human safety consequences. Threshold tuning must be done with local data, not default values from a model zoo.

### Regulatory / Compliance Context

**Niger — Loi n°2021-003 relative à la protection des données à caractère personnel (HAPDP).**
This is Niger's primary data protection law, enforced by the *Haute Autorité de Protection des Données Personnelles* (HAPDP). Key obligations for this system:

- **Lawful basis for biometric processing:** Facial recognition and biometric (fingerprint) data are "sensitive data" under Art. 13 of the law. Explicit consent from data subjects is required, or processing must fall under one of the specific legal exemptions (employment security, public security mandate). The platform must support consent capture (BAS-32: consent signage module with timestamped proof).
- **Data minimization (Art. 5):** Face embeddings stored for access control must be deleted when the individual's employment/authorization ends. Auto-expiration of credentials (D-25) aids compliance. Snapshots of blacklisted individuals must have a defined retention policy.
- **Right of access and erasure (Art. 17-18):** BAS-34 (self-service portal for data subject rights) is a regulatory requirement, not a nice-to-have. Subjects must be able to see what data is held and request deletion.
- **Data transfer restrictions (Art. 38):** Biometric data may not leave Niger without adequate protection. This aligns with the 100% on-premise architecture — no cloud dependency means no unauthorized cross-border transfer.
- **Processing register (Art. 31):** BAS-31 (processing register with CSV/PDF export) is required for the data controller (client organization) to demonstrate compliance during inspection.

**Secondary frameworks that may apply (client-dependent):**
- **ECOWAS Supplementary Act on Personal Data Protection (2010):** Regional framework harmonizing data protection across ECOWAS states. If the client operates across borders (e.g., Niger + Benin + Côte d'Ivoire), this act requires equivalent protections.
- **ONVIF Profile A/C/D/M (access control conformance):** Non-regulatory but functionally required for interoperability with existing door controllers, readers, and credential formats. The system must not break ONVIF conformance for the access control hardware it interfaces with via MQTT.

## 2. Framework Decision

**Selected Framework:** Existing AI Preprocessor Stack — FastAPI + YOLO/ByteTrack + InsightFace + Qdrant

**Version:**
- FastAPI 0.115.0
- ultralytics >= 8.4 (YOLOv12)
- supervision >= 0.25 (ByteTrack)
- insightface >= 0.7.3 (ArcFace)
- onnxruntime >= 1.17.0
- qdrant-client >= 1.18
- opencv-python >= 5.0

**Rationale:**
This is a computer vision pipeline, not an LLM agent system. The existing stack is already built, integrated into the NestJS/BullMQ/Socket.IO pipeline, and purpose-optimized for real-time frame-by-frame processing — exactly what BASTION requires. Adding any LLM/agent framework would introduce 50-500ms+ of unnecessary abstraction overhead per frame, directly contradicting the sub-second alerting constraint. All 31 design decisions (D-01 through D-31) already target this stack, including D-10's explicit choice of InsightFace/ArcFace over Ollama for face recognition.

**Alternatives Considered:**

| Framework | Ruled Out Because |
|-----------|------------------|
| OpenAI Agents SDK | Cloud-tied, OpenAI lock-in, introduces LLM latency to a CV pipeline |
| Anthropic Agent SDK | Claude-only, cloud dependency, overkill for frame-by-frame detection |
| LangChain / LangGraph | Abstraction overhead (50-500ms+ per frame), not designed for real-time CV |
| LlamaIndex | RAG/retrieval framework — wrong system type |
| CrewAI | Multi-agent orchestration — adds complexity to a single-model pipeline |
| Google ADK | Vertex AI lock-in, Gemini dependency, cloud-tied |
| AutoGen / AG2 | Multi-agent conversational — incompatible with CV pipeline architecture |

**Vendor Lock-In Accepted:** No — all components are open-source (MIT/Apache), self-hosted, local-only inference. The explicit choice of InsightFace over Ollama (D-10) reinforces the no-vendor-lock-in position.

The **core detection pipeline** (routes/detection.py) implements the entry point for frame processing:

```python
# ── Single-frame processing pipeline (simplified) ─────────────────
@router.post("/detect")
async def detect_objects(request: DetectionRequest):
    loop = asyncio.get_running_loop()
    frame = _decode_base64_image(request.image_base64)       # PIL → np.array RGB

    # 1. YOLOv12 object detection (CPU-bound → executor)
    detections_sv = await loop.run_in_executor(
        None, lambda: detect(frame, confidence=request.confidence)
    )

    # 2. Zone filtering (supervision PolygonZone)
    zone = sv.PolygonZone(polygon=polygon, frame_resolution_wh=(w, h))
    in_zone = zone.trigger(detections=detections_sv)
    detections_sv = detections_sv[in_zone]

    # 3. ByteTrack cross-frame tracking
    detections_tracked = track(detections_sv)                # Adds tracker_id

    # 4. Temporal smoothing (3-of-last-5 frames rule)
    detections_tracked = _apply_smoothing(detections_tracked, camera_id)

    # 5. Face recognition on best person crop (optional)
    face_match = None
    if enable_face_recognition:
        crop = _extract_person_crop(frame, detections_tracked)
        face_match = await loop.run_in_executor(None, _run_face_rec, crop)

    # 6. Notify NestJS (fire-and-forget via httpx)
    await _notify_nestjs(results, camera_id, org_id)

    return DetectionResponse(
        detections=results, processing_time_ms=elapsed, ...
    )
```

### Key Abstractions

| Concept | What It Is | When You Use It |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| `YOLO` (ultralytics) | Lazy-loaded YOLOv12 model singleton. Produces `ultralytics.engine.results.Results` per frame. | Every frame detection call. Configured via `settings.DETECTION_MODEL` (default `yolov12n`). |
| `sv.Detections` (supervision) | Unified detection data class: `.xyxy`, `.confidence`, `.class_id`, `.tracker_id`. Converters for YOLO, SAM, Detecron2, etc. | Pipeline glue — all stages (filter, track, zone, annotate) operate on this type. Created via `sv.Detections.from_ultralytics()`. |
| `ByteTrack` / `ByteTrackTracker` | Kalman-filter-based cross-frame tracker. Assigns persistent `tracker_id` to objects across frames. | After detection, before temporal smoothing. Configuration: `track_activation_threshold=0.25`, `lost_track_buffer=30`, `minimum_matching_threshold=0.8`. |
| `FaceAnalysis` (insightface) | Detects faces + extracts 512-d L2-normalised embeddings (ArcFace). Uses `buffalo_l` model pack. Call `.get(crop_bgr)` → list of faces with `.normed_embedding`. | Face recognition pipeline. Initialised with `CPUExecutionProvider`, `ctx_id=-1` for CPU-only. |
| `QdrantClient` | Vector DB client for face embedding storage + cosine-similarity search. | Storing enrolled face embeddings; searching against whitelist at runtime (though current pipeline uses in-memory `_whitelist_cache` synced from NestJS). |
| `sv.PolygonZone` | Defines a spatial polygon in frame coordinates. Returns boolean mask: which detections fall inside it. | Zone-based filtering (e.g., only alert on persons entering a restricted zone). Linked to CameraDoorMap zones. |

### Common Pitfalls

1. **InsightFace CPU Execution Provider must be explicit** (`ctx_id=-1` + `providers=["CPUExecutionProvider"]`). If omitted, insightface defaults to CUDA and crashes on CPU-only hardware. The Docker image is `python:3.11-slim` with no CUDA toolkit, so this mismatch silently fails at runtime. (GitHub issue: deepinsight/insightface#1891)

2. **ByteTrack from `supervision`, NOT the standalone `bytetrack` PyPI package.** The `bytetrack` PyPI package (v0.0.1) is a different, unmaintained project. Always import via `from supervision import ByteTrack`. In supervision >=0.28, `ByteTrack` is deprecated in favour of `ByteTrackTracker` from the `trackers` package (`pip install trackers`).

3. **ONNX Runtime version conflicts with insightface.** insightface >=0.7.3 pins `onnxruntime>=1.17.0` but `onnxruntime` and `onnxruntime-gpu` conflict. On CPU-only systems, ensure only `onnxruntime` (not `-gpu`) is installed. Multiple conflicting ONNX packages in the same venv cause silent model loading failures.

4. **YOLO model loading is NOT async-safe.** `YOLO(model_name)` downloads model weights on first call (synchronous I/O). Running it inside `run_in_executor` is safe, but the first request per pod will take 3-10s (cold start). Mitigation: pre-download in Dockerfile or use `ultralytics.settings` to cache weights.

5. **InsightFace model download requires internet at first call.** The `buffalo_l` model (~200 MB) is downloaded automatically by `FaceAnalysis.__init__()`. If the Docker container has no internet access at runtime (air-gapped deployment), pre-download in Dockerfile:
   ```dockerfile
   RUN python -c "from insightface.app import FaceAnalysis; FaceAnalysis(name='buffalo_l', providers=['CPUExecutionProvider'])"
   ```

6. **supervision `PolygonZone` requires integer polygon coordinates.** Passing float coordinates from zone definitions (which use normalised or high-precision floats) causes silent no-match results. Always `np.array(polygon_verts, dtype=np.int32)` before creating a `PolygonZone`.

## 4. Implementation Guidance

### Model Configuration

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **Detection Model** | `yolov12n` (nano) | Balances speed (~2ms on GPU, ~30ms on CPU) vs. accuracy for real-time surveillance. Larger models (s/m/l/x) add latency without meaningful recall gains on constrained camera angles. |
| **Detection Confidence** | `0.45` | Default threshold from `config.py`. For weapons (BAS-03): raise to `0.6` per D-21 to minimise false positives. Configurable per organisation. |
| **Face Match Threshold** | `0.48` Cosine similarity | Mapped to risk score 85/100 (D-11). Range clamped to `[0.20, 0.95]` by `FaceRecognitionRequest.clamp_threshold()`. Higher = fewer false matches but more missed recognitions. |
| **Min Face Size** | `80 px` | Crops smaller than 80×80 px produce unreliable embeddings. Also serves as a distance heuristic — faces further away won't match. |
| **Track Activation Threshold** | `0.25` | ByteTrack default. Detections below this confidence are used in second-pass matching only. |
| **Lost Track Buffer** | `30 frames` | At 15 fps = 2 seconds of occlusion tolerance before track ID is reassigned. |
| **Minimum Matching Threshold** | `0.8` | IoU threshold for matching tracks with detections. Higher = fewer ID switches but more track fragmentation on fast movement. |
| **Dark Frame Threshold** | Mean pixel < `20` | Skips face recognition on near-dark frames where embeddings are garbage. Guard against false positives from noise. |

### Core Pattern: Frame-by-Frame CV Pipeline

The system processes one frame at a time through a deterministic pipeline. All CPU-bound model inference runs via `asyncio.get_running_loop().run_in_executor()` to avoid blocking the FastAPI event loop (T-02-04 mitigation).

```python
import asyncio
import time
import cv2
import numpy as np
import supervision as sv
from fastapi import APIRouter

from app.config import settings
from app.models.detector import get_detector, detect                 # YOLOv12
from app.models.tracker import get_tracker, track                     # ByteTrack
from app.models.face_recogniser import (
    get_face_recogniser, match_whitelist, is_dark_frame,
    refresh_whitelist_cache, prune_stale_buffers,
)

router = APIRouter()
loop = asyncio.get_running_loop()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# STAGE 1: Decode frame (PIL → np.ndarray RGB)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
frame = _decode_base64_image(request.image_base64)  # ~5-15ms

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# STAGE 2: YOLOv12 object detection (CPU-bound → executor)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
detections_sv = await loop.run_in_executor(
    None, lambda: detect(frame, confidence=request.confidence)       # ~30-200ms CPU
)
# Returns sv.Detections with .xyxy, .confidence, .class_id

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# STAGE 3: Zone filtering (supervision PolygonZone)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
if request.detection_zones:
    in_any_zone = np.zeros(len(detections_sv), dtype=bool)
    for polygon_verts in request.detection_zones:
        polygon = np.array(polygon_verts, dtype=np.int32)           # Must be int32! (Pitfall 6)
        zone = sv.PolygonZone(polygon=polygon,
                              frame_resolution_wh=(w, h))
        in_any_zone |= zone.trigger(detections=detections_sv)
    detections_sv = detections_sv[in_any_zone]

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# STAGE 4: ByteTrack cross-frame tracking (adds tracker_id)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
detections_tracked = track(detections_sv)                           # ~1-5ms

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# STAGE 5: Temporal smoothing (≥3 of last 5 frames rule)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# Filters out transient false positives: a detection must persist
# across ≥3 frames within a 5-frame rolling window to be reported.
frame_buffer = get_frame_buffer()                                    # Global in-memory dict
for i, tid in enumerate(detections_tracked.tracker_id):
    key = (request.camera_id, int(tid))
    buf = frame_buffer.setdefault(key, [])
    buf.append(True)
    if len(buf) > 5:
        buf.pop(0)
    if sum(buf) < 3:
        smoothing_mask[i] = False                                   # Unstable → discard
detections_tracked = detections_tracked[smoothing_mask]

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# STAGE 6: Face recognition (optional, on best person crop)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
if request.enable_face_recognition and not is_dark_frame(frame):
    best_person = _find_best_person(detections_tracked)
    if best_person is not None:
        x1, y1, x2, y2 = map(int, best_person.xyxy)
        # Expand bbox 20% upward for better face capture
        y1 = max(0, y1 - int((y2-y1) * 0.2))
        crop_bgr = cv2.cvtColor(frame[y1:y2, x1:x2], cv2.COLOR_RGB2BGR)

        face_result = await loop.run_in_executor(
            None, _run_face_rec_on_crop, crop_bgr, settings.FACE_MATCH_THRESHOLD
        )
        # Returns (identity_id, identity_name, similarity) or None

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# STAGE 7: Notify NestJS (fire-and-forget) & return response
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
await _notify_nestjs(results, camera_id, org_id, timestamp,
                     face_matches=face_matches_list)

prune_stale_buffers()  # Clean tracking buffers >30s stale

return DetectionResponse(detections=results, processing_time_ms=elapsed, ...)
```

### Context Window Strategy (Adapted for CV Pipeline)

For CV pipelines, "context window" maps to temporal frame history:

| Concept | CV Equivalent | BASTION Strategy |
|---------|--------------|------------------|
| **Token limit** | Frame sampling rate | Process every frame (continuous). For high-fps cameras (>15), consider adaptive sampling — skip frames when no motion detected. |

| **Dark frame skip** | Pre-filtering | Mean pixel < 20 → skip face recognition entirely. Saves inference cost and prevents garbage embeddings. |

### 4b.1 Structured Outputs with Pydantic

#### Validation & Retry Pattern

Unlike LLM structured outputs (where retry means re-prompting), CV pipeline validation failures indicate corrupt input data, not logic errors:

```python
def _decode_base64_image(image_base64: str) -> np.ndarray:
    """Decode base64 → PIL → np.ndarray. 0 retries — fail fast on corrupt input."""
    try:
        image_data = base64.b64decode(image_base64)
        image = Image.open(io.BytesIO(image_data))
        return np.array(image.convert("RGB"))
    except Exception as e:
        logger.error("Failed to decode image: %s", e)
        raise HTTPException(status_code=400, detail="Invalid image data")
```

- **Retries on decode failure: 0** — corrupt frames should not be reprocessed. The caller (NestJS frame capture service) should retry at the camera capture level if needed.
- **Retries on model inference: 0** — each frame is independent. A transient model failure (e.g., OOM) means the whole pod needs restarting, not retrying the frame.
- **Retries on NestJS notify: 1** — the `_notify_nestjs` function logs and swallows errors. The NestJS BullMQ processor has its own retry logic if the alert enqueue fails.
- **What to log:** detection_count, face_match_count, processing_time_ms, error type. Never log raw embeddings or full frame base64 (T-02-06).

### 4b.2 Async-First Design

The AI Preprocessor uses FastAPI's async event loop with synchronous model inference offloaded to thread pools.

#### How Async Works Here

```
FastAPI event loop (uvicorn worker, single thread)
  ├── await loop.run_in_executor(None, detect, frame)   ← YOLO runs in ThreadPoolExecutor
  ├── await loop.run_in_executor(None, _run_face_rec)    ← insightface runs in ThreadPoolExecutor
  ├── await httpx.AsyncClient.post(...)                  ← I/O-bound NestJS notification
  └── await refresh_whitelist_cache()                    ← I/O-bound HTTP fetch
```

All CPU-bound model inference runs via `run_in_executor()` with the default `ThreadPoolExecutor` (min(32, os.cpu_count() + 4) workers). This prevents YOLO inference (30-200ms) from blocking other requests.

#### The One Common Mistake

**Mistake:** Calling `asyncio.run()` inside an async route handler to run synchronous code.

```python
# ❌ WRONG — creates a new event loop inside an existing one
@router.post("/detect")
async def detect_objects(request):
    result = asyncio.run(sync_detect(frame))  # RuntimeError: asyncio.run() cannot be called from a running event loop
```

```python
# ✅ CORRECT — offload to executor
@router.post("/detect")
async def detect_objects(request):
    loop = asyncio.get_running_loop()
    result = await loop.run_in_executor(None, sync_detect, frame)
```

**Second mistake:** Running I/O-bound operations (HTTP requests) in the executor instead of using `httpx.AsyncClient`. This starves the executor thread pool for actual CPU-bound work.

#### When to Stream vs. Await

| Pattern | Use Case | Chosen Approach |
|---------|----------|-----------------|
| `await` (blocking) | Detection pipeline — caller needs the full result before acting | Always. Each frame is processed synchronously through the pipeline. |
| `asyncio.gather()` | Running YOLO + audio classification on the same frame in parallel | Useful for multi-model frames (vision + audio), but not currently implemented. |
| Fire-and-forget (`asyncio.create_task`) | NestJS notification — detection result is returned before the notification completes | Used in `_notify_nestjs()`. Errors logged but never surfaced. |
| Streaming response (`StreamingResponse`) | Not applicable to CV pipeline | CV frames are discrete requests. Streaming would add complexity without benefit. |

### 4b.3 Model Configuration Engineering

For CV pipelines, "prompt engineering" translates to **confidence threshold tuning and model selection architecture**.

#### System vs. User Configuration Separation

Treat model parameters like a system prompt — fixed per deployment, overridable per request:


#### Threshold Strategy (Analogue of Few-Shot/Token Budget)

| Threshold | Effect | BASTION Value | When to Tune |
|-----------|--------|---------------|--------------|
| Detection confidence | Lower = more detections (more FPs). Higher = fewer detections (more FNs). | `0.45` (general), `0.60` (weapons) | Per camera angle — a camera pointing at a gate with a narrow FOV can use lower thresholds than one covering a wide parking lot. |
| Face match threshold | Lower = more matches (more false positives). Higher = fewer matches (more missed recognitions). | `0.48` (mapped to score 85/100) | Per organisation (D-11). Uncertain matches (0.48-0.60) are logged + operator-notified. |
| NMS IoU threshold | Non-Maximum Suppression overlap. Lower = fewer overlapping boxes. | Ultralytics default (0.7) | Not adjusted per frame. If YOLO produces double detections on small objects (distant persons), consider lowering to 0.5. |
| Track activation threshold | Detections below this are "second-pass" only. | `0.25` (ByteTrack default) | Increase to 0.35 in high-traffic areas to reduce track fragmentation. |
| Dark frame mean pixel | Skip face recognition on near-dark frames. | `20` | Adjust per camera — IR cameras may have higher noise floor. |

#### Confidence Calibration

```python
# Map face similarity to risk score (D-11)
# Similarity is cosine distance on L2-normalised embeddings → [-1, 1]
# ArcFace outputs are typically in [0.0, 1.0] for same-person comparisons
def similarity_to_risk_score(similarity: float) -> int:
    """Map cosine similarity to 0-100 risk score."""
    return int(max(0, min(100, (similarity + 1.0) * 50)))

# Threshold zones:
#   85-100: Match            → log + optional auto-unlock
#   60-84:  Uncertain        → log + operator notification
#    0-59:  No match         → ignored
```

### 4b.4 Context Window Management

For CV pipelines, "context window" maps to **temporal frame history** and **tracking state**.

#### Frame Sampling Rate (Token Limit Equivalent)

```python
# Current: every frame (push from NestJS)
# If camera runs at 30 fps, but human movement is ~5 fps:
#
# Adaptive sampling strategy (recommended for Phase 3):
#   1. Compute frame difference (Δ mean pixel or Δ histogram)
#   2. If Δ < threshold → frame is static → skip detection, reuse last result
#   3. If Δ > threshold → frame has motion → run full pipeline
#
# This reduces inference load by 60-80% on static scenes
# while maintaining real-time responsiveness on motion.
def has_motion(frame: np.ndarray, prev_frame: np.ndarray, threshold: float = 15.0) -> bool:
    diff = cv2.absdiff(frame, prev_frame)
    return float(np.mean(diff)) > threshold
```

#### ByteTrack Lost Track Buffer (Conversation History Equivalent)

- **Buffer size:** 30 frames (~2 seconds at 15 fps)
- **Behaviour:** When a tracked object leaves the frame or is occluded, ByteTrack keeps it "alive" for up to 30 frames. If it reappears within that window, the original tracker_id is re-assigned (maintaining identity).
- **BASTION use:** Abandoned object detection (BAS-04) relies on this buffer. An object that stops moving → its track is "lost" → timer starts. If timer exceeds X minutes → alert.
- **Memory bound:** Each active track stores ~350 bytes of Kalman filter state. With max 50 simultaneous tracks per camera × 50 cameras = <1 MB. Not a concern.

#### Qdrant Search Window (RAG Retrieval Equivalent)

- **Current approach:** In-memory brute-force search over all whitelist embeddings (O(n) dot products). Suitable for <10,000 faces.
- **Scaling strategy (for 10,000+ faces):**
  1. Qdrant ANN search on `faces` collection with `limit=10` (O(log n))
  2. Re-rank top-10 with exact cosine similarity
  3. Multi-tenancy: per-organization collection isolation (payload filter on `organization_id`)
- **Embedding dimension:** 512-d float32 (ArcFace). 10,000 faces × 512 × 4 bytes = ~20 MB in RAM.

#### BASTION-Specific Frame Management

| Feature | Frame History Needed | Strategy |
|---------|---------------------|----------|
| **Abandoned object** (BAS-04) | X minutes per zone | ByteTrack lost_track_buffer + per-object timer. Object static for >X min = abandoned. Configurable per zone (D-20). |
| **Loitering** (BAS-06) | Time-in-zone per tracker_id | ByteTrack tracker_id + zone enter/exit timestamps. If time_in_zone > threshold = loitering alert. |
| **Crowd counting** (BAS-05) | Instantaneous count | No history needed — count persons per zone from current frame. Moving average over 5 frames to smooth noise. |
| **Weapons** (BAS-03) | Single frame | No history needed — if YOLO confidence >0.6, alert immediately. |
| **Anti-spoofing** (BAS-02) | Single frame | Passive liveness analysis on single frame. No temporal component. |

## 5. Evaluation Strategy

This section defines how we measure success for the BASTION AI computer vision pipeline. Unlike LLM-based systems, CV models are evaluated through code-based metrics (mAP, precision, recall, F1, latency percentiles) on labeled datasets, not through LLM judges. Human evaluation by domain experts (security operators, system integrators) is essential for the subjective dimensions where operator trust and alarm fatigue determine real-world efficacy.

**No LLM judges are used** — this is a deterministic CV pipeline where every detection has a ground-truth label. Human evaluation focuses on workflow impact and edge case validation, not on scoring individual outputs.

Correct camera chosen per CameraDoorMap priority | Correlated snapshot shows: empty doorway, wrong camera feed, blurry/blown-out frame, or clip missing the crucial moment. Footage lacks metadata for incident investigation or prosecution | **Code:** Snapshot valid (non-blank, correct camera_id, timestamp within 1s of event). Clip duration = 10s ± 1s. Validates snapshot captures the moment of access denial | **Critical** |
| 6 | **Detection Pipeline Latency** | Critical Failure Mode #3 — "Detection pipeline latency exceeding real-time threshold" | End-to-end processing: YOLO detection + ByteTrack tracking + zone filter + alert dispatch completes within 200ms for CPU (target 100ms for GPU). P99 latency <300ms CPU (<200ms GPU). Pipeline does not block camera frame ingestion (frame dropped rather than queued >500ms) | Detection processing time exceeds 500ms for P50 or 1s for P99. Frame pile-up causes latency cascade: one slow frame delays subsequent frames. Operator sees alerts 3+ seconds after the event occurred | **Code:** P50 and P99 processing time per stage (decode, detect, track, zone, notify) measured from `/detect` endpoint. Latency waterfall across 10,000 frames. Number of frames dropped due to timeout | **High** |
| 7 | **Face Recognition Demographic Fairness** | Section 1b — Known Failure Mode #2: demographic bias in deployment region | False match rate and false non-match rate measured and reported separately for each demographic subgroup (Hausa, Zarma, Tuareg, Fulani; with and without head coverings). No subgroup has >2× the FMR or FNMR of the best-performing subgroup. Audit report published at calibration time | African subjects (primary user population in Niger) have 10-100× higher false match rate than lighter-skinned subjects (from original training data). Subjects wearing hijab/turban/tagelmust consistently fail to match | **Code:** FMR/FNMR stratified by demographic attributes (skin tone estimate, head-covering presence, ethnicity if self-reported in enrollment). Measured on a representative calibration set of 500+ locally enrolled subjects. Disparity ratio = subgroup error rate / overall error rate. Target: <2× for any subgroup<br>**Human:** Privacy/compliance officer (client) reviews demographic fairness report before production deployment. Validates no group disproportionately affected | **Critical** |
| 8 | **Abandoned Object False Positive Control** | Section 1b — Known Failure Mode #1: alarm fatigue spiral | Abandoned object detection generates <0.5 false positives per camera per shift. Objects must be static for full configurable threshold (X minutes) before alerting. Transient objects (person stops for 10s, chair moved) are not flagged. Detection triggers only in configured zones | System flags trash bins, parked carts, furniture, persons standing still as "abandoned objects". False positives >1 per camera per shift within first week. Objects that never moved are flagged repeatedly | **Code:** Precision/Recall on abandoned object test set (50+ scenarios with various object types, durations, and zone configurations). FP rate = abandoned alerts that operator dismisses as non-actionable / total abandoned alerts | **Medium** |
| 9 | **Crowd Counting Accuracy** | Section 1b (BAS-05 derived) | Person count per zone is within ±2 of ground truth for scenes with <30 people. For scenes >30 people, count is within ±20% of ground truth. Counting updates at frame rate (no lag). Density % calculation matches zone area measurement | Person count is off by >5 for small crowds or >50% for large crowds. Double-counting of same person by adjacent zones. Lag causes crowd reports to show pre-evacuation headcount | **Code:** Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of count vs. ground truth across 200+ frames with varied crowd densities. Count consistency across a 30-frame window (std dev should be <3 for static crowds) | **Medium** |

## 6. Guardrails

Guardrails are classified as **Online** (run on every detection, real-time, catastrophic if wrong) or **Offline** (batch analysis, feeds continuous improvement, quality signal). For CV pipelines, guardrails are primarily confidence thresholds and rejection rules — not content moderation filters used in LLM systems.

The guiding principle: **prevent catastrophe first, improve quality over time.**

### Online (Real-Time) — Applied to Every Detection

| # | Guardrail | Trigger Condition | Intervention | Latency Budget | Rationale |
|---|-----------|-------------------|--------------|----------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Weapon confidence floor** | YOLO weapon class (firearm, knife) confidence < `0.30` | **Flag for human review** — do not alert but store detection in review queue with snapshot. Detection at 0.30-0.59 logged as "low confidence potential weapon" for operator to inspect at leisure | +0ms (same inference pass, just a threshold check) | Prevents silent false negatives at very low confidence while avoiding false positives that cause alarm fatigue. Per D-21, the alert threshold is 0.60; the floor is 0.30. Below 0.30, the signal is too weak even for review |
| 2 | **Anti-spoofing liveness rejection** | Liveness score < `0.30` for any face detection | **Reject face recognition result** — do not match against whitelist or blacklist. Log the detection as "spoof attempt" with snapshot. If score between 0.30-0.60 → uncertain → log + notify operator | +15-30ms (liveness inference adds minimal overhead) | Prevents a printed photo or screen replay from triggering a blacklist match (which could escalate to armed confrontation). Per D-15 and domain rubric, passive liveness must reject presentation attacks with >90% confidence |
| 3 | **Blacklist match escalation** | Face match score > blacklist threshold (default 0.48, configurable) AND liveness score > 0.60 | **Escalate — CRITICAL alert to ALL operators** + push notification + immutable audit entry with snapshot and chain-of-custody hash. Door lockdown trigger if auto-lock enabled | +0ms (decision follows face match result) | Per D-12, blacklist matches carry human safety consequences. Cannot be suppressed or rate-limited. Every blacklist match generates an auditable incident |
| 4 | **Dark frame skip** | Frame mean pixel luminance < `20` | **Skip face recognition entirely** for this frame. Log frame as "dark — face skipped" without processing | +1ms (pixel mean calculation) | Prevents garbage embeddings from near-dark IR frames that would cause false face matches. Frame is still processed for YOLO detection (weapons, persons) where IR imaging is functional |
| 5 | **Max per-frame processing time** | Pipeline processing exceeds `500ms` for any single frame | **Drop the frame** — return a timeout response to NestJS. Do not queue the frame. Increment `frames_dropped_timeout` counter. Log frame_id, camera_id, elapsed time | +0ms (timer-based abort) | Prevents latency cascade: one slow frame blocking subsequent frames. The caller (NestJS frame capture) retries on the next camera frame. Aligned with critical failure mode #3 |
| 6 | **Min face size rejection** | Detected face bounding box < `80px` on shortest side | **Reject face recognition** for this face. Do not attempt embedding extraction or matching. Log as "face too small" | +0ms (bbox dimension check) | Crops <80px produce unreliable embeddings (per Section 4 configuration). Prevents false non-matches (wasted compute) and false matches (noise) |
| 7 | **Zone-restricted detection** | Weapon or abandoned object detected outside any configured trigger zone | **Suppress alert** — log the detection with metadata but do not send notification. Detection still appears in timeline search | +0ms (zone filter stage already runs) | Prevents alerts from non-critical areas (public sidewalks, distant parking edges) where the detection is unlikely to be actionable. Reduces operator noise |

### Offline (Flywheel) — Batch Analysis, Quality Improvement

| # | Metric | Sampling Strategy | Analysis Cadence | Action on Degradation |
|---|--------|-------------------|------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Weapon detection false positive rate** | Sample 100% of weapon alerts from the past 24 hours. Compare against operator dismiss/no-action rate | **Daily** — automated report to security operations manager | If FP rate > 10% (more than 1 in 10 weapon alerts dismissed as non-actionable): flag for per-camera threshold tuning. If FP rate > 25%: escalate to VaultOS engineering for model review |
| 2 | **Face recognition false non-match rate** | Sample 20% of all "no match" face detections where operator confirms (via access log or manual review) that the person should have been recognized | **Weekly** — automated comparison of access event personnel vs. face recognition matches | If FNMR > 15%: trigger whitelist embedding refresh (re-extract embeddings from enrollment photos with updated model version). If FNMR > 25%: flag for demographic fairness audit |
| 3 | **Anti-spoofing false rejection rate (BPCER)** | Sample 20% of "spoof rejected" face detections where operator can visually confirm the subject is a genuine live person | **Weekly** — operator reviews sampled rejection snapshots | If BPCER > 5%: adjust liveness score threshold. Log the rate by camera model — some legacy cameras may produce lower-quality images that trigger false rejection. Add per-camera liveness threshold override if needed |
| 4 | **Abandoned object false positive rate** | Sample 100% of abandoned object alerts from past 24 hours. Classify as genuine (action taken) or nuisance (dismissed) | **Daily** during incubation mode (first 72h), then weekly | If FP rate > 20%: increase minimum static duration per zone. If specific zones are responsible for >50% of FPs: disable abandoned object detection for those zones and re-evaluate zone geometry. See known failure mode #1 |
| 5 | **Operator action rate (alert quality signal)** | Rate of alerts that result in any operator action: dispatch guard, lockdown door, call police, acknowledge with investigation | **Weekly** — trend analysis across all cameras and sites | If action rate drops below 50% for any camera: flag for threshold review. If site-wide action rate drops below 30%: schedule operator retraining and system recalibration. Divergence from baseline >20% triggers investigation |
| 6 | **Detection latency drift** | P50 and P99 processing time from all frames in past 24 hours | **Daily** — automated alert if thresholds breached | If P50 > 300ms or P99 > 500ms: check CPU utilization, memory pressure, and concurrent request count. Escalate to system administrator if CPU > 90% sustained. Investigate new model version if deployed within 48h |
| 7 | **Demographic fairness drift** | FMR/FNMR stratified by demographic attributes on weekly accumulated face detection data | **Weekly** — automated fairness report with disparity ratios per subgroup | If any subgroup has disparity ratio > 2.0×: flag for human review. If disparity > 3.0×: block auto-unlock feature for that site until recalibrated. Report shared with compliance officer per HAPDP Art. 31 |
| 8 | **Incubation mode automatic calibration** | First 72 hours: all detections logged but only CRITICAL (blacklist, weapon >0.6) generate alerts. All other detections sampled for threshold tuning | **One-time** — runs automatically at deployment on each new site | After 72h: generate calibration report with recommended per-camera thresholds based on observed false positive rate. Operator reviews and approves before system exits incubation mode. Per-domain expert recommendation in Section 1b Known Failure Mode #1 |

## 7. Production Monitoring

Production monitoring for a CV pipeline is fundamentally different from LLM system monitoring. There is no "conversation trace" to visualize — instead, we monitor detection throughput, latency distributions, confidence score trends, and operator behavior patterns. All monitoring builds on the structured detection logs stored in PostgreSQL via the NestJS `DetectionLogService`.

**Tracing Tool:** Structured JSON detection logs in PostgreSQL + Prometheus metrics + Grafana dashboards.

Unlike LLM systems where Arize Phoenix or Langfuse provide conversation traces, this CV pipeline's individual detections are high-volume, low-semantic-density events (hundreds of thousands per day). A specialized tracing tool would add cost and complexity without commensurate insight. The observability strategy is:

1. **Every detection event** is logged as a JSONB row in `detection_logs` table with: camera_id, timestamp, model_version, detection_count_by_class, confidence_histogram, processing_time_ms, face_match_results, zone_hits, error flags
2. **Prometheus metrics** exposed by NestJS `/api/metrics`: detection_throughput_total, detection_latency_seconds (p50/p90/p99), frames_dropped_total, alerts_generated_total, operator_action_total
3. **Grafana dashboard** visualizes: per-site detection health, latency waterfall, confidence distribution trends, operator action rate trends, fairness drift

---

### Key Metrics to Track

| # | Metric | Why It Matters | Source | Granularity |
|---|--------|---------------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | **Detection throughput** (frames/sec per camera) | Dropping below expected fps indicates frame capture or AI Preprocessor pipeline issue | Prometheus counter from NestJS | Per camera, 1-minute avg |
| 2 | **Pipeline latency** (P50, P90, P99 in ms) | Latency spikes predict frame pile-up and missed alerts | `processing_time_ms` from each `/detect` response, recorded in `detection_logs` | Per pipeline stage, 5-minute buckets |
| 3 | **Weapon detection confidence distribution** (mean, P10, P90 per day) | Gradual confidence decline suggests model drift or environmental degradation (dust, IR degradation known in Section 1b Infrastructure Failure #3) | `confidence` field stored per weapon detection | Per camera, daily |
| 4 | **False positive rate trend** (nuisance alerts per camera per shift) | Rising FP rate is the earliest indicator of alarm fatigue spiral (known failure mode #1 — the most common deployment killer) | Computed from operator dismissal rate on alerts | Per camera, daily rolling 7-day avg |
| 5 | **Operator action rate** (% of alerts that trigger action) | Dropping action rate signals operator desensitization. Target >80% in month 1, >60% sustained | Computed from alert events where operator dispatched/lockdown vs. dismissed | Per site, weekly rolling average |
| 6 | **Frames dropped due to timeout** (count per hour) | Rising drop rate indicates the pipeline cannot keep up with camera frame rate | `frames_dropped_timeout` Prometheus counter | Per camera, 5-minute buckets |
| 7 | **Face recognition throughput** (match attempts/sec) | Ensures face recognition is running (not silently disabled or crashed) | Prometheus counter from face_recognition route | Per site, 1-minute avg |
| 8 | **Anti-spoofing rejection rate** (% of facedet rejected as spoof) | Sudden spike may indicate: (a) camera degradation causing false rejections, (b) real spoof attack in progress, (c) model file corruption | Computed from liveness_score across all face detections | Per camera, 1-hour buckets with threshold alert >20% change from 24h baseline |

### Alert Thresholds

| Alert | Condition | Severity | Response |
|-------|-----------|----------|----------|

| **Frame drop rate > 10%** | More than 10% of frames dropped per camera per hour | **P1 (Critical)** | Automatic page. Check camera stream health, network packet loss, and AI Preprocessor concurrent request pool |
| **Weapon recall degradation** | Weapon detection confidence P50 drops > 0.15 below baseline (deployment-day baseline) over a 7-day rolling window | **P2 (High)** | Flag to system integrator. Schedule camera lens cleaning (dust accumulation is a known issue per Section 1b Infrastructure Failure #3). Verify IR illuminator output |
| **Operator action rate decline** | Operator action rate drops by >20% from 7-day baseline and stays below threshold for 3 consecutive days | **P2 (High)** | Notify security operations manager. Schedule operator feedback session and system recalibration. Check if any recent threshold change contributed |
| **Demographic disparity ratio > 2.0×** | Weekly fairness analysis shows any subgroup with >2× the FMR/FNMR of best-performing group | **P2 (High)** | Generate fairness report. Flag to VaultOS product owner. Disable auto-unlock for affected site until recalibrated. Prepare HAPDP compliance notification |

| **No detections from a camera** | Zero detections from any camera for >10 minutes (camera is alive per stream health check) | **P2 (High)** | Check AI Preprocessor log for errors. Verify model loading. Check route connectivity between NestJS and AI Preprocessor |

If >10 false blacklist matches in 1 hour, auto-temporarily disable blacklist matching and notify all operators |

### Smart Sampling Strategy

For human review, we cannot review every detection — the pipeline processes millions of frames daily. Instead, we use signal-based sampling to select the most informative subset:

| Signal | Sample Rate | What We Review | Why |
|--------|-------------|----------------|-----|
| **High-confidence weapon detection** (>0.60) | 100% — every alert | Snapshot + bounding box overlay | These are the most consequential: an armed person entering or a false positive that could have escalated. Must validate each one |
| **Uncertain weapon detection** (0.30-0.60) | 20% stratified by camera | Snapshot + confidence score + operator outcome | These are the calibration frontier. Reviewing them helps tune per-camera thresholds |
| **Spoof-rejected face** (liveness <0.30) | 10% stratified by camera and time of day | Snapshot of the rejected face + liveness score heatmap | Validates that anti-spoofing is correctly rejecting attacks vs. falsely rejecting genuine users. Higher sample rate in first week of deployment (50%) for calibration |
| **Uncertain face match** (0.48-0.60 similarity) | 25% of detections | Comparison: enrollment photo vs. detection snapshot side-by-side + operator rating | These boundary-case matches determine the threshold calibration. If operators consistently confirm matches in this band, threshold should be lowered |
| **Blacklist match** | 100% — every single one | Full incident package: snapshot before/during/after, liveness score, match score, operator response audit | Human safety consequences. Every blacklist match is independently verified and audited per HAPDP Art. 31 processing register requirements |
| **Operator-dismissed alert** (action=ignore within 5s) | 10% per camera per day | Snapshot + alert type + reason if operator commented | Direct feedback on false positive quality. If any camera has >50% dismissal rate, increase to 50% sample until threshold tuning is done |
| **Random ambient sample** | 0.1% of all frames (unconditional) | Detection results + confidence distribution + latency | Baseline for signal-metric divergence. Without random sampling, we only see the edge cases. This sample tells us what "normal" looks like — and detects silent degradation before it triggers alerts |
| **Fairness audit sample** | All face detections from targets who self-reported ethnicity at enrollment (with consent) | Match outcome + score + demographic tag | Required for weekly demographic fairness analysis. Only aggregated statistics, never individual identification, stored outside the eval dataset |

**Sampling weights during incubation mode (first 72 hours):**
- All sample rates increased 3× (or to 100% where feasible) during the initial calibration period
- Every uncertain detection logged but not alerted is reviewed by the deployment integrator
- Calibration report generated at hour 72 includes: recommended per-camera thresholds, observed FP rates, observed FN rates, and a fairness baseline

**Data retention for monitoring:**
- Detection logs: 90 days in PostgreSQL, then aggregated to daily statistics and archived (raw logs purged)
- Sampled review snapshots: 30 days for non-alert detections, 1 year for alert-causing detections (compliance)
- Fairness audit aggregates: retained for the duration of the processing register (HAPDP compliance)
- All retention configurable per site via BAS-26 (now extended from VISION retention settings)

### Incident Response Protocol

When any **P1 alert** fires:

1. **Immediate:** AI Preprocessor automatically reduces frame processing rate by 50% (adaptive sampling) to prevent total pipeline collapse. Critical alerts continue at full rate.
2. **Within 5 minutes:** On-call engineer acknowledges page. Checks: (a) CPU/memory on AI Preprocessor host, (b) recent deployment or model change, (c) camera stream health.
3. **Within 15 minutes:** Decision: rollback model version, restart pod, or reduce camera frame rate. If latency P99 > 1s: rollback to last-known-good model.
4. **Within 1 hour:** Root cause identified. Review entry added to deployment log. Alert thresholds adjusted if the incident was a monitoring false alarm.
5. **Within 24 hours:** Post-mortem written. If the incident involved missed detections or false blacklist matches: HAPDP processing register updated. Operator retraining scheduled if alarm fatigue contributed.

This protocol follows the criticality established in Section 1b: in physical security, a system that silently degrades is worse than no system at all, because operators develop a false sense of safety.
